1000 Women in Irish History

The Knowledge Graph for Irish History offers a new and dynamic way to discover the interconnected histories of people and places in Ireland, from the middle ages to the twenty-first century. By weaving together individual threads of data, we can now experience Irish history through connections linking people across political, economic, social, and cultural life in Ireland.

The Knowledge Graph brings together data from many different sources. One of these sources is the Dictionary of Irish Biography (DIB), an authoritative reference work of data containing biographies of the most notable figures in Irish history.

“The DIB is a project of the Royal Irish Academy. It tells the island's life story through the biographies, at home and overseas, of prominent men and women born in Ireland, north and south, and the noteworthy Irish careers of those born outside of Ireland.”

As part of a collaboration between Beyond 2022 and The Dictionary of Irish Biography, we explore the representation of individuals in the DIB dataset through a set of interactive visualisations. This visual story experiments with new ways of representing and understanding a large and complex historical data set and discovers how historical data can shape our views of the past.

What's in the data?

Firstly, let's take a look at the data.

The visualisation below shows a bird's-eye view of the Knowledge Graph for Irish History. The inner circles show various groups of data in the knowledge graph, called "subgraphs". The size of each circle shows the number of entities (such as individual people or places) contained in each subgraph.

With unique person entities and rich metadata for thousands of people and places, the Knowledge Graph helps us reveal and explore the shared stories that make up our history.

By splitting the DIB subgraph by gender, we can see there are many more entries about men than women. Let’s focus on the women contained in the DIB subgraph. This visualisation shows all the women within the DIB subgraph. Each circle represents an individual woman.

Explore Areas Of Interest

In the DIB, "areas of interest" are used to categorise historical figures, often grouping them together according to particular themes or subjects. The visualisation below shows the frequency of all "areas of interest" associated with the women in the DIB subgraph.

Each circle represents a different category. The size of the circle tells us how many women fall under that area of interest. As you can see, the most common areas of interest for the women in the DIB are Literature, Politics, Religion, Arts and Crafts.

These categories can also tell us about a person's social status or the type of career they held, helping researchers to search for individual biographies or specific groups of people. Women whose area of interest is tagged as Literature were likely writers or poets.

Other tags are harder to categorise. For instance, there are three women associated with an Area of Interest labelled The Sea. One is Gráinne O'Malley, who was a sea captain. Another is Annie Letitia Massy, who was a marine biologist. But the last one is Louisa Coppin, a little girl whose ghost reportedly predicted the location of Sir John Franklin`s lost polar expedition!

The visualisation below shows areas of interest for both men and women in the DIB subgraph. The size of the circle tells us the total number of people with that area of interest. The blue circles indicate how many people in each area of interest are men. The yellow circles indicate how many people are women. Do you notice anything new?

Areas of interest historically dominated by men occur more often in this visualisation. This is because historians of the past focused more on notable men than notable women in Irish history. This focus still shapes how we understand the past, including the categories we use to document it. For example, having an area of interest for The Sea makes more sense knowing that it primarily contains male shipbuilders, or men in the navy.

Next, let's take a closer look at the women in the DIB dataset. This visualisation shows the distribution of areas of interest associated with women across many centuries. The size of the circle indicates how frequently that area of interest appears in that time period. If you click on a circle, you can explore instances of that area of interest across time.

We can see there is an explosion of new areas of interest in the twentieth century. During this time, the growth of education, industry and technology opened new doors for women, helping them to access more public, well-documented roles in Irish life. This helps to explain why most biographies for women within the DIB come from the twentieth century.

Explore Occupations

The DIB subgraph also contains lots of information about another category: occupations. This category provides more detailed information than areas of interest: it can tell us a person's job, title or the main role they were known for in life.

The visualisation below shows the frequency of all occupations held by women in the DIB subgraph. Each circle represents a different occupation. The size of the circle tells us how many women held that occupation. Note that the most frequent occupations match the most frequent areas of interest we saw above!

Occupations give us a lot of data. We can connect this data to specific women in the DIB subgraph. For example, see if you can zoom in to find the circle labelled "King of Leinster". The woman attached to this occupation was named Aife. Aife was the daughter of Diarmait Mac Murchada, king of Leinster in the twelfth century. However, Aife was not the king of Leinster herself; if we check her DIB biography, we discover that her occupation was probably meant to read "daughter of the king of Leinster".

The next visualisation shows the occupations for both men and women in the DIB subgraph. Again, the size of the circle tells us each occupation's total frequency in the subgraph. The blue circles indicate how many people in each occupation are men. The orange circles indicate how many people are women.

Here, we see the sheer amount of rich information contained in just one dataset!. We can explore thousands of lives across Irish history through the diverse range of careers identified by DIB biographers. Some occupations show up frequently through the centuries, like the 804 men and 39 women listed as Politicians. Other occupations are unique to individuals. Edward Fossett, for example, is the only person documented as a Circus Owner in the DIB.

Zoom in and see what interesting occupations you can find!

This visualisation shows us how the types of occupations women held have changed over time. We can see that the 20th century has the broadest amount of occupations. Again, this is because the DIB has more records on women from the 20th century than from other time periods. However, note which occupations appear in multiple time periods and which ones appear only once. Unique occupations can teach us about individuals and the time period they lived in. You can explore the occupations by clicking on them to see which are the most frequently recorded!

Now that you've had a chance to discover the richness of the DIB subgraph in the Knowledge Graph for Irish History, why not explore further? All of the individual women who make up the data-points in these visualisations have individual biographies and fascinating stories recorded in the Dictionary of Irish Biography. Discover Alice Kyteler, accused of witchcraft in the fourteenth century, or Grainne O'Malley, the sixteenth-century sea captain. Perhaps you're interested in Christian Davis (alias Mother Ross), a soldier and adventurer, or Margaret Linday Huggines, the pioneer astrophysicist. You can discover all of their stories at dib.ie.

Alternatively, you can explore all of these individual histories in the Knowledge Graph for Irish History, which contains many thousands of entities - people, places, offices and organisations - from more than seven centuries of Irish history. Lodview allows you to explore the metadata associated with these people and places, and discover the connections that linked them.
